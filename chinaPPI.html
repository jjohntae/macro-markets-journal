China’s Missing Oil Demand: A Temporary Solution or a Delayed Problem?

Published June 11 2026

One of the most interesting developments during the Strait of Hormuz disruption has been China’s decision to dramatically reduce crude oil imports.

Before the conflict, China imported roughly 11 million barrels of crude oil per day, including approximately 1.3 to 1.4 million barrels per day from Iran. China accounted for nearly 90% of Iran’s oil exports, making it by far Iran’s most important customer. Since the outbreak of the conflict, however, China’s crude imports have fallen to roughly 7.8 million barrels per day.

This decline is significant because China is the world’s largest crude oil importer. When a country of that size suddenly reduces imports by several million barrels per day, the effects are felt far beyond its own borders.

Many analysts initially expected a prolonged disruption of the Strait of Hormuz to send oil prices significantly higher as Middle Eastern exports fell. Instead, oil prices remained far below those forecasts. One possible explanation is that part of the lost supply was effectively offset by weaker Chinese demand.

China reduced imports, drew down inventories, expanded electric vehicle usage, increased reliance on high-speed rail, and lowered refinery utilization rates. As a result, global oil markets faced not only a supply shock, but also a decline in demand. In other words, part of the reason oil prices remained relatively stable may be that the world’s largest oil importer simply consumed less oil.

At first glance, this appears to be a positive development. Lower Chinese demand helped offset part of the supply disruption and prevented oil prices from rising as much as many analysts expected.

However, I began to wonder whether this adjustment was creating costs elsewhere in the economy.

A significant portion of China’s crude oil imports is not used for transportation fuel. Instead, it is processed into petrochemical feedstocks such as ethylene, plastics, packaging materials, and other industrial inputs. When refiners and petrochemical producers reduce operating rates, oil demand falls, but so does the production of these materials.

This is why China’s recent increase in Producer Price Index (PPI) caught my attention.

China’s PPI rose 3.9% year-over-year in May, suggesting that cost pressures may be building within the manufacturing sector. In other words, some of the pressure that would normally appear through higher oil prices may instead be showing up through higher production costs.

What I found particularly interesting is that China’s lower imports may not necessarily reflect a permanent decline in demand. According to recent reports, many refiners appear to be relying on inventories instead of purchasing expensive crude on the spot market. If that is the case, current import figures may understate China’s true demand for oil.

This distinction matters because inventories can postpone demand, but they cannot eliminate it.

China may have successfully reduced imports in the short run, but if industrial activity recovers and inventories need to be replenished, refiners may eventually have to return to the market and increase purchases. In that scenario, the recent decline in imports could prove temporary rather than structural.

For this reason, I believe one of the most important signals to watch is whether China returns to the spot market in the coming months. If Chinese refiners begin increasing purchases while Middle Eastern supply remains constrained, global oil markets could become tighter than they appear today.

My prediction is that markets may be underestimating how much current stability depends on temporarily weaker Chinese demand. If China’s industrial activity normalizes and inventory drawdowns become less sustainable, the next major move in oil prices may come not from a new supply disruption, but from the return of demand.
