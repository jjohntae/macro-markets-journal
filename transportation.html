The Strait of Hormuz: How a Supply Shock Reshaped Global Oil Markets

Published June 8 2026

Recent economic data continue to suggest that the U.S. economy remains more resilient than many investors expected. In May, nonfarm payrolls increased by 172,000, more than double the consensus estimate of roughly 80,000, while the unemployment rate remained at 4.3%. In addition, average job growth over the past three months reached approximately 188,000, marking one of the strongest sustained hiring periods since 2024.

To me, these figures matter because the labor market is one of the clearest leading indicators of economic activity. As long as businesses continue hiring and consumers continue earning income, demand for goods and services should remain healthy. That translates into stronger freight volumes, trucking demand, rail shipments, and logistics activity, all of which directly benefit transportation companies.

The stronger-than-expected labor data also shifted investors’ expectations for monetary policy. With economic growth remaining resilient, markets have become less confident that the Federal Reserve will need to cut interest rates aggressively in the near term. While higher rates can pressure valuations, they also reinforce the view that underlying economic activity remains solid.

At the same time, geopolitical developments have provided an additional tailwind. Following President Trump’s decision to cancel planned strikes on Iran and growing expectations for a ceasefire, oil prices fell below $90 per barrel. Since fuel represents one of the largest operating expenses for trucking, airlines, and shipping companies, lower energy costs can meaningfully improve operating margins while resilient economic growth continues to support revenue.

These dynamics help explain why the Dow Jones Transportation Average has risen roughly 13.8% over the past month. Transportation stocks have historically served as a barometer of economic activity, and the sector’s recent outperformance suggests that investors are increasingly positioning for a stronger-than-expected growth environment.
