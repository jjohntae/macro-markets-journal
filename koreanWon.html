Understanding Why the Korean Won Is Weak and What it Means for Korean Stocks

Published June 27 2026

Recently, the Korean won depreciated to its weakest level since 2009, prompting me to revisit what I had learned about exchange rates in my macroeconomics course.

When Korea announced its fiscal stimulus package, my first thought was whether an increase in government spending, and eventually M2, had contributed to the currency’s depreciation. In macroeconomics, we often learn that an expanding money supply can place downward pressure on a currency.

However, after reading recent market commentary and research, I realized that the current weakness of the won appears to be driven less by domestic liquidity and more by global capital flows.

According to the Korea Economic Institute of America, portfolio outflows have become a more important driver of the won than trade itself. Although South Korea recorded a USD 101.8 billion current account surplus between January and November 2025, Korean residents invested an even larger USD 129.4 billion overseas during the same period. As a result, outward investment exceeded foreign currency earned through trade by approximately USD 27.6 billion, forcing the Bank of Korea to draw on its foreign exchange reserves. This suggests that the exchange rate is increasingly being shaped by capital allocation decisions rather than export performance alone.

A major reason for these capital outflows is the growing return advantage offered by U.S. financial assets. According to The Wall Street Journal, the U.S. dollar reached a one-year high after markets increasingly expected the Federal Reserve to raise interest rates later in the year.

Higher U.S. interest rates increase the return available on dollar-denominated assets, particularly U.S. Treasury securities. Combined with strong expectations for U.S. economic growth and continued investment in AI, these higher yields encourage global investors to allocate more capital to U.S. financial assets.

The U.S. 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.57%, while the 30-year Treasury yield climbed above 5%, compared with Korea’s 4.27% 10-year government bond yield. These higher returns encourage investors, including Korean households and institutions, to shift more capital into U.S. assets. As more investors make this conversion, demand for dollars increases while demand for won declines, placing downward pressure on the Korean currency. In this way, portfolio allocation decisions are transmitted directly into the foreign exchange market.

From a macroeconomic perspective, the recent depreciation of the Korean won demonstrates that exchange rates are more than just a measure of a country’s currency—they directly influence international investment returns. While Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix continue to benefit from strong AI-driven demand and have supported gains in the KOSPI, foreign investors evaluate Korean assets differently from domestic investors.

Because international investors ultimately measure their performance in their home currency, their realized return depends not only on the stock’s performance but also on movements in the exchange rate. Consequently, even if Korean equities generate strong returns in won terms, continued depreciation of the won can substantially reduce those gains once converted back into U.S. dollars. As a result, foreign investors are not simply asking whether Samsung Electronics or SK Hynix are good companies. They are asking whether their expected returns are high enough to compensate for continued weakness in the Korean won.

If depreciation pressures persist, Korean companies may need to deliver even stronger earnings growth and stock performance to remain competitive with investment opportunities in other markets.

What initially seemed like a simple question about money supply ultimately became a lesson in how investor expectations and capital flows can dominate exchange-rate movements in the short run.
