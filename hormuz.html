The Strait of Hormuz and What It Revealed About Global Oil Markets

Published June 8 2026

Market Analysis

Following the escalation of the conflict in the Middle East, markets grew increasingly concerned that the conflict could disrupt shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, threatening one of the world’s most critical routes for crude oil exports. The Strait of Hormuz is one of the world’s most important energy chokepoints, with roughly 25% of global seaborne oil trade passing through it. As tanker traffic through the region became increasingly restricted, the May 2026 IEA Oil Market Report characterized the situation as an “unprecedented supply shock,” noting that more than 14 million barrels per day of oil supply had been temporarily shut in while cumulative supply losses exceeded one billion barrels. To put the magnitude into perspective, the volume of supply removed from the market was equivalent to roughly two-thirds of total U.S. daily petroleum consumption, which averaged 20.6 million barrels per day in 2025. Although Gulf countries’ total oil exports recovered by 1.9 million barrels per day in April to 10.5 million barrels per day, the IEA noted that exports still remained 14 million barrels per day below February levels. Total Middle East Gulf oil production, including Qatar, was down 14.4 million barrels per day, or 45%, from the start of the war, falling to 17.6 million barrels per day. The immediate impact of this supply shock became particularly visible in the physical crude market. Dated Brent, the benchmark price for physical crude cargoes available for immediate delivery, began trading at a premium of nearly $35 per barrel over Brent futures, which reflect expectations about future oil prices. This widening premium reflected the market’s willingness to pay significantly more for oil available today than for oil delivered in the future, highlighting that the immediate challenge was not the existence of oil itself, but the availability of physical barrels that could actually be delivered. From a macroeconomic perspective, this illustrates how prices function as an allocation mechanism under conditions of scarcity. Although global oil reserves had not suddenly disappeared, disruptions to transportation and logistics reduced the effective supply available to the market. As a result, buyers placed a higher value on immediately deliverable barrels, causing spot prices to rise relative to futures prices. In other words, the market was pricing not only the quantity of oil, but also the ability to physically move that oil to where it was needed. This suggests that commodity markets do not simply price underlying resources; they also price the flexibility and resilience of global supply chains. This disruption did not affect every region equally. Many Asian economies rely heavily on Middle Eastern crude transported through the Strait of Hormuz, whereas the United States and, to a lesser extent, Europe have access to more diversified sources of supply. As Gulf exports declined, Asian buyers were forced to compete aggressively for alternative barrels from producers outside the region. The resulting increase in regional fuel prices created strong economic incentives for traders to redirect cargoes over unusually long distances. As a result, higher supplies from producers outside the region led to a surge in shipments from the Atlantic Basin to East of Suez markets, with Atlantic Basin exports increasing by 3.5 million barrels per day since the start of the conflict. Bloomberg reported that traders began sending diesel cargoes from Europe to Australia and from the U.S. Gulf Coast to Asia because fuel prices in those markets had risen enough to offset the substantial transportation costs. Rather than simply reflecting a shortage of oil, price signals actively reshaped global trade flows by pulling available barrels toward the regions where they were valued most highly. The United States played an especially important role as an alternative supplier. U.S. crude and condensate exports were roughly flat in March at 3.9 million barrels per day, but then surged to a record 5.2 million barrels per day in April. Around 900 thousand barrels per day of that increase went to OECD Asia-Pacific, with shipments to Japan averaging close to 600 thousand barrels per day. This helps explain why U.S. crude became more important in the global market during the disruption. The scale of this market-driven rerouting was significant. Global supply losses were partly offset by higher flows from the Atlantic Basin, where exports increased by 3.5 million barrels per day since the start of the war. The United States, Kazakhstan, Russia, and Venezuela provided most of the increase. This is important from a market perspective because it demonstrates that prices do not merely reflect supply and demand conditions; they actively reshape global trade flows by directing physical barrels toward the markets where they are valued most highly. However, the adjustment was not enough to fully offset the shock. Even after these replacement flows, global crude exports in April were still 6.6 million barrels per day lower than February levels. On the demand side of the physical crude market, China cut crude arrivals by 3.6 million barrels per day from February to April, while Japan, Korea, and India reduced imports by 1.9 million, 1.0 million, and 0.76 million barrels per day, respectively. Together, these four countries cut crude imports by 7.2 million barrels per day, which the IEA describes as around three-quarters of Gulf crude export losses. To me, this was one of the most interesting parts of the report. The market did not balance only through new supply. It also balanced through import cuts, inventory draws, and reduced refinery demand. That means the apparent stabilization in prices did not necessarily reflect a fully solved supply problem. It reflected a combination of temporary buffers. What I found most interesting was that prices did not simply react to the disruption. They became the mechanism through which the market reorganized global supply chains. The oil itself had not disappeared; rather, the economics of moving those barrels had fundamentally changed. In that sense, the episode demonstrated that commodity markets are not merely pricing mechanisms–they are coordination mechanisms that determine where scarce physical resources flow.
