The Strait of Hormuz: How a Supply Shock Reshaped Global Oil Markets

Published June 8 2026

Market Analysis

Following the escalation of the conflict in the Middle East, markets grew increasingly concerned that the conflict could disrupt shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, threatening one of the world’s most critical routes for crude oil exports. The Strait of Hormuz is one of the world’s most important energy chokepoints, with roughly 25% of global seaborne oil trade passing through it. As tanker traffic through the region became increasingly restricted, the May 2026 IEA Oil Market Report characterized the situation as an “unprecedented supply shock,” noting that more than 14 million barrels per day of oil supply had been temporarily shut in while cumulative supply losses exceeded one billion barrels. To put the magnitude into perspective, the volume of supply removed from the market was equivalent to roughly two-thirds of total U.S. daily petroleum consumption, which averaged 20.6 million barrels per day in 2025.

Although Gulf countries’ total oil exports recovered by 1.9 million barrels per day in April to 10.5 million barrels per day, the IEA noted that exports still remained 14 million barrels per day below February levels. Total Middle East Gulf oil production, including Qatar, was down 14.4 million barrels per day, or 45%, from the start of the war, falling to 17.6 million barrels per day.

The immediate impact of this supply shock became particularly visible in the physical crude market. Dated Brent, the benchmark price for physical crude cargoes available for immediate delivery, began trading at a premium of nearly $35 per barrel over Brent futures, which reflect expectations about future oil prices. This widening premium reflected the market’s willingness to pay significantly more for oil available today than for oil delivered in the future, highlighting that the immediate challenge was not the existence of oil itself, but the availability of physical barrels that could actually be delivered.

From a macroeconomic perspective, this illustrates how prices function as an allocation mechanism under conditions of scarcity. Although global oil reserves had not suddenly disappeared, disruptions to transportation and logistics reduced the effective supply available to the market. As a result, buyers placed a higher value on immediately deliverable barrels, causing spot prices to rise relative to futures prices. In other words, the market was pricing not only the quantity of oil, but also the ability to physically move that oil to where it was needed. This suggests that commodity markets do not simply price underlying resources; they also price the flexibility and resilience of global supply chains.

This disruption did not affect every region equally. Many Asian economies rely heavily on Middle Eastern crude transported through the Strait of Hormuz, whereas the United States and, to a lesser extent, Europe have access to more diversified sources of supply. As Gulf exports declined, Asian buyers were forced to compete aggressively for alternative barrels from producers outside the region. The resulting increase in regional fuel prices created strong economic incentives for traders to redirect cargoes over unusually long distances. As a result, higher supplies from producers outside the region led to a surge in shipments from the Atlantic Basin to East of Suez markets, with Atlantic Basin exports increasing by 3.5 million barrels per day since the start of the conflict. Bloomberg reported that traders began sending diesel cargoes from Europe to Australia and from the U.S. Gulf Coast to Asia because fuel prices in those markets had risen enough to offset the substantial transportation costs. Rather than simply reflecting a shortage of oil, price signals actively reshaped global trade flows by pulling available barrels toward the regions where they were valued most highly.

The United States played an especially important role as an alternative supplier. U.S. crude and condensate exports were roughly flat in March at 3.9 million barrels per day, but then surged to a record 5.2 million barrels per day in April. Around 900 thousand barrels per day of that increase went to OECD Asia-Pacific, with shipments to Japan averaging close to 600 thousand barrels per day. This helps explain why U.S. crude became more important in the global market during the disruption.

The scale of this market-driven rerouting was significant. Global supply losses were partly offset by higher flows from the Atlantic Basin, where exports increased by 3.5 million barrels per day since the start of the war. The United States, Kazakhstan, Russia, and Venezuela provided most of the increase. This is important from a market perspective because it demonstrates that prices do not merely reflect supply and demand conditions; they actively reshape global trade flows by directing physical barrels toward the markets where they are valued most highly.

However, the adjustment was not enough to fully offset the shock. Even after these replacement flows, global crude exports in April were still 6.6 million barrels per day lower than February levels. On the demand side of the physical crude market, China cut crude arrivals by 3.6 million barrels per day from February to April, while Japan, Korea, and India reduced imports by 1.9 million, 1.0 million, and 0.76 million barrels per day, respectively. Together, these four countries cut crude imports by 7.2 million barrels per day, which the IEA describes as around three-quarters of Gulf crude export losses.

To me, this was one of the most interesting parts of the report. The market did not balance only through new supply. It also balanced through import cuts, inventory draws, and reduced refinery demand. That means the apparent stabilization in prices did not necessarily reflect a fully solved supply problem. It reflected a combination of temporary buffers.

What I found most interesting was that prices did not simply react to the disruption. They became the mechanism through which the market reorganized global supply chains. The oil itself had not disappeared; rather, the economics of moving those barrels had fundamentally changed. In that sense, the episode demonstrated that commodity markets are not merely pricing mechanisms–they are coordination mechanisms that determine where scarce physical resources flow.

Market Trend

The dynamics observed during the Strait of Hormuz disruption may also point to a broader structural shift in global energy markets. According to S&P Global Vice Chairman and energy historian Daniel Yergin, the conflict represents “a defining moment for world energy” and will lead to “a rethinking and new emphasis on energy security and resilience.” Rather than relying solely on the lowest-cost sources of supply, governments and firms appear increasingly willing to diversify suppliers, expand strategic inventories, and invest in alternative production regions to reduce geopolitical risk. The article further notes that many energy companies are “waiting to see where prices go before making new commitments,” suggesting that geopolitical uncertainty itself has become an important factor shaping investment decisions. Even when prices are sufficiently high to justify additional production, firms may delay irreversible capital expenditures until the long-term outlook becomes clearer. In this sense, the market is not merely pricing the quantity of oil available today; it is increasingly pricing the strategic value of flexibility, diversification, and supply chain resilience.

Macroeconomic Implications

One of the broader economic implications of the Strait of Hormuz disruption is its effect on inflation expectations and monetary policy. Higher oil prices do not only affect energy companies—they also increase transportation, logistics, and production costs across the economy. As these costs are passed on to consumers, inflationary pressures become more widespread.

Recent U.S. inflation data illustrate this transmission mechanism. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, the Consumer Price Index rose 3.8% year-over-year in April 2026, up from 3.3% in March, while the energy index increased 17.9% and gasoline prices rose 28.4% over the same period. Although the initial shock originated in the oil market, its effects extended into broader consumer prices.

For policymakers, sustained inflation is a concern because it suggests that price pressures are becoming more entrenched throughout the economy rather than remaining confined to energy markets. When inflation remains elevated, central banks are generally less willing to lower interest rates, as cheaper borrowing could further stimulate demand and intensify inflationary pressures. This creates a difficult tradeoff for central banks. Lower interest rates stimulate borrowing, investment, and consumption, but they can also increase aggregate demand and place additional upward pressure on prices. Conversely, keeping interest rates elevated helps restrain spending and contain inflation, although it may also slow economic growth.

This tension helps explain the recent shift in market expectations regarding U.S. monetary policy. Concerns about persistent inflation have been reinforced by stronger-than-expected employment data, which suggest that economic demand remains resilient. Reuters reported that Goldman Sachs had pushed back its forecast for the Federal Reserve’s first rate cut from late 2026 to 2027. The report noted that the Fed is likely to wait until “the effects of tariffs, higher oil prices linked to the Iran conflict and other war-related pressures fade, and until year-over-year core PCE inflation [, which excludes volatile food and energy prices and is closely watched by the Federal Reserve,] moves closer to the 2% target, alongside a cooling in what it views as overstated AI-driven demand.”

Under normal circumstances, persistently high inflation, elevated energy prices, and prolonged restrictive monetary policy would be expected to slow economic activity and increase the risk of stagflation. However, one factor helping offset these pressures has been the exceptionally strong AI-driven investment cycle. The rapid expansion of data centers, semiconductor manufacturing, and broader electronics supply chains has generated substantial capital spending despite higher energy costs and tighter monetary conditions. South Korea and Taiwan have recorded export growth rates of roughly 40-50% year-over-year, while Malaysia, Singapore, and Vietnam have posted gains of 15-35%, reflecting the broad-based strength of the electronics ecosystem. In addition, the conflict itself has increased demand for products tied to energy security, including generators, batteries, electric vehicles, and defense equipment.

According to DBS Chief Economist Taimur Baig, the more surprising development following the Iran conflict has been “the resilience of growth.” While traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has declined by approximately 90-95% over the past three months and prices for many petroleum-related products have risen between 20% and 100% this year, economic activity across much of Asia has remained unexpectedly robust. Consequently, the global economy has avoided the severe stagflationary slowdown that many analysts initially feared, despite continued inflationary pressures and elevated energy prices.

However, the resilience of growth has also reduced the urgency for monetary easing. With inflation remaining above target and economic activity proving stronger than expected, investors increasingly expect interest rates to remain elevated for longer. Financial markets have reflected this shift in expectations. As investors reduced expectations for near-term Federal Reserve rate cuts, Treasury bond prices declined and yields rose, with the benchmark 10-year Treasury yield climbing above 4.6%, up from below 4.0% before the Iran conflict began. This rise in yields is significant because Treasury securities serve as a benchmark for borrowing costs throughout the economy. Higher Treasury yields translate into higher mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, and financing expenses for capital-intensive projects such as AI data centers. At the same time, rising yields make bonds relatively more attractive than equities and increase the discount rate used to value future corporate earnings. As a result, equity valuations, particularly in high-growth technology sectors that rely heavily on future cash flows, came under pressure. Reflecting these concerns, the S&P 500 fell 0.7%, the Nasdaq Composite declined 0.8%, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average lost more than 320 points on May 20.

Taken together, these developments suggest that two powerful macroeconomic forces are operating simultaneously. On one hand, the energy shock is creating persistent inflationary pressures that encourage central banks to maintain restrictive monetary policy. This dynamic is increasingly reflected in financial markets, where stronger-than-expected employment data and elevated inflation have pushed Treasury yields higher as investors price in a “higher-for-longer” interest-rate environment. On the other hand, strong AI-related investment demand and resilient global trade are supporting economic activity, reducing the urgency for monetary easing. Rather than producing the classic 1970s-style combination of high inflation and collapsing growth, the current environment appears to be characterized by elevated inflation alongside surprisingly resilient investment and output. Financial markets appear to be increasingly pricing in this outcome through higher Treasury yields and reduced expectations for near-term monetary easing.

Investment Implications: Energy Infrastructure as a Strategic Asset

The changing macroeconomic environment has also altered the way investors view the energy sector itself. Traditionally, higher oil prices have led investors to focus primarily on upstream producers such as Exxon Mobil, Chevron, and Devon Energy, whose earnings rise directly with commodity prices. However, the Strait of Hormuz disruption demonstrated that markets were increasingly pricing not only the value of oil, but also the ability to securely transport, store, and deliver physical energy supplies.

As discussed earlier, the market adjustment occurred not simply through higher production, but through a large-scale rerouting of global trade flows. Atlantic Basin exports increased by 3.5 million barrels per day, while U.S. crude exports surged to a record 5.2 million barrels per day as buyers sought alternative suppliers. This suggests that energy infrastructure itself has become a strategic asset. If governments and firms continue to diversify supply chains and reduce dependence on geopolitically vulnerable regions, the value of pipelines, storage facilities, LNG terminals, and export networks may continue to increase.

This broader theme may help explain why Morningstar recently highlighted several midstream companies, including MPLX, Cheniere Energy, and Oneok, as attractive opportunities. In its analysis of MPLX, Morningstar emphasized the company’s strategy of building a “full value chain for shippers, from well-to-water.” Rather than relying solely on oil prices, MPLX participates across the entire logistics network—from gathering production at the wellhead, through pipelines and processing facilities, to export terminals that connect North American energy supplies with overseas markets.

Unlike traditional exploration and production companies, many midstream operators generate relatively stable fee-based cash flows through long-term transportation agreements. As long as energy volumes continue moving through their systems, these firms collect transportation and processing fees regardless of short-term fluctuations in commodity prices. This business model may become particularly attractive in an environment characterized by persistent inflation, elevated interest rates, and increasing emphasis on energy security.

Moreover, many energy infrastructure companies provide relatively high dividend yields, making them attractive during periods of higher interest rates and inflation. Morningstar notes that energy stocks can serve as a hedge against inflation because oil and gas prices often rise alongside broader price levels. Combined with stable contracted cash flows and real asset exposure, midstream operators may offer investors a way to benefit not only from higher commodity prices, but also from the growing strategic importance of resilient global energy supply chains.
